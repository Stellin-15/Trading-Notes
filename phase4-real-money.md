# Phase 4 Notes — Going Live with Real Money

Reference sheet for Month 6+. This is the last phase of the roadmap — where [Phase 3](phase3-paper-trading.md)'s discipline meets real capital and real consequences. The goal here isn't to make money fast; it's to survive long enough to let a real edge compound.

---

## 1. Gate Criteria — Are You Actually Ready?

Don't go live just because you're impatient or bored with paper trading. Go live when you can honestly check every box:

- **Track record**: 50-100+ paper trades (ideally across trending, ranging, and volatile conditions — not just one lucky month), with documented, consistently positive expectancy in your journal.
- **Discipline, not just profit**: you followed your own entry/exit/stop rules consistently — not "mostly," consistently. A profitable paper record built on broken rules is not evidence of readiness; it's evidence you got lucky.
- **Drawdown tolerance tested**: you've been through at least one real losing streak in paper trading and didn't abandon or rewrite your rules mid-stream.
- **Emotional baseline**: you can explain, in plain language, why you took every trade — no "gut feeling" entries.

If any of these is shaky, that's not a failure — it's information. Go back to Phase 3 and put in more reps before risking capital.

---

## 2. Start Small — Much Smaller Than You Think

This is the single most important adjustment, and the one beginners resist the most.

- **Reduce size dramatically versus your paper trading.** If you were comfortably trading $1,000 positions in the simulator, start live with something like $100 positions — not because the strategy changed, but because *you* haven't been tested with real money yet.
- **Expect a performance dip.** Almost everyone's win rate and profit factor drop when they switch from paper to real money — the psychological weight of real dollars changes decision-making even when the rules on paper are identical. This is normal, not a sign your strategy is broken.
- **Position sizing rule stays the same as paper trading**: risk 1-2% of your actual account per trade. On a $2,000 account, that's a $20-$40 max loss per trade — small numbers, but the point right now is proving you can execute the process under real conditions, not maximizing profit.
- **Match your account size to what you can actually afford to lose entirely.** Trading is not something to fund with money you need for rent, bills, or emergencies.

---

## 3. Choosing a Broker

For a US-based beginner in stocks/equities, look for: $0 commissions, strong regulation (FINRA/SIPC-covered), good educational resources, and a paper trading feature so you can keep testing new ideas without extra accounts. Commonly recommended for beginners:

- **Fidelity** — widely rated the best all-around beginner broker: $0 commissions, strong free research/education, no account minimums, fractional shares.
- **Charles Schwab** — $0 commissions on stocks/ETFs/options, thinkorswim platform (same one from Phase 3's paperMoney) carries over so your paper-trading environment becomes your live one, strong customer service.
- **Webull** — free stock/ETF trades, fast mobile setup, though generally rated behind Fidelity/Schwab on educational depth and fee transparency on some products.

Whichever you pick, confirm it's regulated (FINRA/SIPC in the US) — this is non-negotiable, not a nice-to-have.

## 4. The Psychology of Real Money (Where Most People Actually Fail)

A good strategy with poor psychology loses money. A mediocre strategy with excellent discipline can still be profitable. This is the part that paper trading can only partially prepare you for.

- **Overconfidence after wins** — a common, account-destroying pattern: after a few winning trades, a trader increases position size beyond their own plan, feeling emboldened. Then one normal-sized loss, taken at an oversized position, wipes out several wins' worth of gains. **Guard**: position size changes should be a deliberate, pre-planned decision (see Scaling below), never an in-the-moment reaction to a hot streak.
- **Revenge trading and overtrading** — same failure modes flagged in Phase 3, but with real stakes now the pull is stronger. The reset rule still applies: after a loss or a rule violation, force a pause rather than immediately trying to "win it back."
- **Position sizing as your emotional thermostat** — if a trade's size is making you check the price every five minutes or feel physically stressed, it's too big for you right now, regardless of what the 1-2% math says is "allowed." Dial size down until you can watch a trade play out calmly.
- **Separate feelings from decisions** — set your entry/exit/stop rules *before* opening a position, and let the plan — not the current emotional state — execute the trade.

---

## 5. Scaling Up — Only After Sustained Live Profitability

- Increase position size gradually, and only after a sustained period of live profitability (not after one good week or a lucky streak). A common approach: only scale up after you've accumulated enough live track record to be confident the edge — not variance — is driving results.
- Scaling decisions should follow the same rule-based discipline as everything else: define in advance what "sustained profitability" means for you (e.g., X consecutive profitable months, or a live sample size of Y trades with maintained expectancy) before you allow yourself to increase size.
- Never scale up to "make back" a loss — that's revenge trading with extra steps.

---

## 6. The Continuous Improvement Loop

Trading live doesn't mean the learning stops — treat it as an ongoing cycle back through the earlier phases:

- **Keep journaling** — same discipline as Phase 3, now with real P&L. Rule adherence still matters more than any single trade's outcome.
- **Periodically re-backtest** ([Phase 2](phase2-backtesting.md)) as market conditions shift — a strategy tuned for a trending market may need re-validation if the market turns choppy for months.
- **Avoid strategy-hopping** — the urge to abandon a strategy after a normal losing streak (instead of a genuine, data-supported failure) is one of the fastest ways to never build an edge. Distinguish "this strategy has a bad week" from "this strategy's expectancy has genuinely broken down" using your metrics, not your mood.

---

## Quick Self-Check (test yourself before/after starting live)
1. Why should your first live position sizes be smaller than what you traded comfortably in paper trading?
2. What's the difference between judging live readiness by profit versus judging it by discipline — and why does discipline matter more at the gate?
3. Describe the overconfidence-after-wins pattern and the guard against it.
4. What should trigger a decision to scale up position size — and what should never trigger it?
5. How do you tell the difference between "this strategy is having a normal bad week" and "this strategy has actually stopped working"?

If you can answer all five and you've met the gate criteria in Section 1 — you have the full arc: newbie → foundations → strategies → backtesting → paper trading → live, with a loop back to keep refining as you go.
