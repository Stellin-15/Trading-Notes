## 1. How to Actually Read an Earnings Report

A quarterly earnings report typically includes: revenue, net income, EPS (both GAAP — standard accounting — and "adjusted"/non-GAAP figures), forward guidance, management commentary, and supporting tables (cash flow, balance sheet highlights — see Phase 0's Accountant's Lens for what these mean).

**The headline numbers everyone watches**:
- **EPS beat/miss** — did actual EPS come in above or below what analysts expected? This is the most-watched number, but it can be skewed by share buybacks (fewer shares outstanding inflates EPS even with flat profit) or one-off accounting items — don't take it at face value alone.
- **Revenue beat/miss** — signals actual demand strength or weakness for the business, generally considered a "cleaner" number than EPS since it's harder to engineer.

**The number that actually moves the stock more than the ones above**: **guidance** (the company's own forecast for the next quarter/year). The market cares more about what a company says it will do than what it just did.

**Key combinations to recognize**:
- Beat on revenue + EPS + **raised** guidance → usually a strong rally.
- Beat on revenue + EPS but **lowered/weak** guidance → stock can still sell off hard, even on a "good" quarter. This is why stocks sometimes drop right after "beating earnings" — the market was already pricing in the beat and is reacting to the outlook instead.
- Miss on headline numbers but **stronger-than-expected guidance** → can produce a muted reaction or even a rally, despite the "miss" headline.

**Practical takeaway**: before reacting to an earnings headline, check whether guidance moved up or down — that's usually the real story.

## 2. Sector Rotation

**Sector rotation** = money flowing out of one part of the market and into another, driven by where we are in the business cycle, interest rates, and institutional risk appetite. Institutional investors don't hold the same sectors through the whole business cycle — it's described well as "you wouldn't wear a parka in July."

**How interest rates drive rotation** (extends Phase 0's Fed/interest-rate section):
- **Rates rising** → money tends to rotate toward **financials** (banks can charge more for loans, so higher rates often help them) and **energy**. Higher rates hit **high-growth tech** the hardest — their valuations rely heavily on future earnings, and higher rates reduce the present value of those future profits.
- **Economy slowing / recession fears** → money rotates toward **defensive sectors**: utilities, healthcare, consumer staples — businesses that keep generating revenue regardless of the economic climate.

**A leading signal to watch**: the **yield curve** — the gap between short-term and long-term interest rates. An **inverted yield curve** (short-term rates higher than long-term) has historically preceded rotations into defensive sectors and, often, recessions. Worth knowing what it is even if you don't trade off it directly.

**Top-down approach**: professional sector analysis starts broad and narrows down — overall monetary policy and interest rate environment → which phase of the business cycle we're likely in → which sectors historically do well in that phase → then individual stock selection within that sector. This connects directly to Phase 1's "fundamentals overlay" — checking sector strength before trusting a technical signal is a simplified version of this same top-down logic.

**Important nuance**: the stock market tends to move *ahead of* the actual economic cycle, because investors are pricing in what they expect to happen, not just what has already happened. This is why stocks can rally "in a recession" or fall "in a good economy" — the market already priced in the shift earlier.

## 3. Putting It Together with Your Roadmap

- When you see an earnings beat/miss headline (news, social media, etc.), your first move should be: check guidance, not just the beat/miss headline.
- When picking which sector to focus your Phase 1 strategies on, ask: where are we in the rate cycle (rising, falling, paused) and what does that typically favor? Then check if the sector you're looking at is actually showing relative strength against the S&P 500 (Phase 1's sector-strength check).
- None of this replaces your technical entry rules — it's a filter to apply *before* you even start looking for a Phase 1 setup, same as the fundamentals overlay was framed.
