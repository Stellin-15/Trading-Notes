# Phase 0 Notes — Money, Markets & Trading Fundamentals

Reference sheet for Week 1. Organized by lens (accountant → financier → economist → trader) since the same word can mean slightly different things depending who's using it.

---

## 1. The Accountant's Lens (how a business's numbers work)

Trading a stock means buying a piece of a business — you need to read its numbers.

**The three core statements**
- **Balance Sheet** — snapshot at one moment: `Assets = Liabilities + Equity`
  - **Assets** — things the company owns (cash, inventory, buildings, equipment)
  - **Liabilities** — what it owes (loans, unpaid bills)
  - **Equity (Shareholders' Equity)** — what's left for owners after paying off liabilities. This is literally what your shares represent a slice of.
- **Income Statement (P&L)** — performance over a period:
  - **Revenue** — total money from sales (the "top line")
  - **Expenses** — cost of running the business
  - **Net Income ("the bottom line")** — Revenue − Expenses = profit
- **Cash Flow Statement** — tracks actual cash moving in/out (a company can show "profit" on paper but still run out of cash — this statement catches that)

**Key ratios/terms you'll see quoted about a stock**
- **EPS (Earnings Per Share)** — Net Income ÷ number of shares outstanding. Higher = each share "earns" more.
- **P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)** — Stock Price ÷ EPS. Rough shorthand for "how expensive is this stock relative to its profit." High P/E = market expects strong future growth (or it's overpriced); low P/E = cheap (or something's wrong).
- **Book Value** — Assets − Liabilities, i.e. equity per share. A very rough "floor" value if the company were liquidated.
- **Dividend** — a cash payment a company makes to shareholders from profits, usually quarterly. Not all companies pay one (growth companies often reinvest instead).
- **Market Cap** — Share Price × Total Shares Outstanding = what the market thinks the whole company is worth. Determines size category:
  - Large-cap (>$10B), Mid-cap ($2B-$10B), Small-cap (<$2B) — smaller caps = more volatile, less liquid.

## 2. The Financier's Lens (how money grows, risk, and structure)

**Time value of money**
- **Present Value / Future Value** — a dollar today is worth more than a dollar next year because it can be invested/earn interest. This underlies almost all valuation.
- **Compounding** — earning returns on your returns. Small consistent gains compound dramatically over years — this is why long-term investing and disciplined trading both lean on compounding, not one big win.
- **Interest Rate** — the cost of borrowing money / the return for lending it. Central to everything (see Economics section — the Fed sets a baseline rate that ripples through all markets).

**Risk & return**
- **Risk vs Return** — higher potential return generally requires accepting higher potential loss. There's no free lunch; if something promises high return with "no risk," be suspicious.
- **Volatility** — how much and how fast a price swings. Measured statistically (standard deviation) but practically: a volatile stock can make or lose you a lot fast.
- **Beta** — a stock's volatility *relative to the overall market* (market beta = 1.0). Beta of 1.5 means it tends to move 50% more than the market, up or down.
- **Diversification** — spreading money across different assets so one bad outcome doesn't wipe you out. "Don't put all your eggs in one basket."
- **Liquidity** — how easily an asset can be bought/sold without moving its price much. Large, popular stocks (Apple) are highly liquid; small obscure stocks are not (harder to exit a losing trade fast).
- **Leverage** — using borrowed money to increase position size (see Margin below). Amplifies both gains AND losses — the #1 way beginners blow up accounts.
- **Margin** — a loan from your broker to buy more stock than your cash covers. If the trade goes against you, you can get a **margin call** (forced to add cash or the broker liquidates your position). Avoid margin entirely as a beginner.

**Asset types / instruments**
- **Stocks (Equities)** — ownership shares in a company.
- **Bonds** — a loan you make to a company/government; they pay you interest (**yield**) and return principal at maturity. Generally lower risk/return than stocks.
- **ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)** — a basket of stocks/bonds bundled into one tradable ticker (e.g., SPY = tracks the S&P 500). Great way to get instant diversification.
- **Index** — a benchmark basket representing a market segment (S&P 500 = 500 large US companies, NASDAQ = tech-heavy, Dow Jones = 30 large industrial companies).
- **Options** — a contract giving the right (not obligation) to buy/sell a stock at a set price by a set date. Advanced — not touching this until stocks are solid.

**Portfolio concepts**
- **Asset Allocation** — how you split money across stocks/bonds/cash/etc. based on risk tolerance and goals.
- **Position Sizing** — how much of your account you put into a single trade. THE single biggest lever for surviving as a trader (more in Phase 3).
- **Drawdown** — the drop from a peak in your account value to a subsequent low. "Max drawdown" is a key metric for how painful a strategy's worst stretch is.

## 3. The Economist's Lens (the forces moving the whole market)

**Macro basics**
- **Supply & Demand** — the root of all pricing. More buyers than sellers at a price → price rises, and vice versa. Stock prices are just supply/demand for shares in real time.
- **Inflation** — the general rise in prices over time / fall in purchasing power of money. Measured by CPI (Consumer Price Index). Moderate inflation is normal; high inflation erodes real returns.
- **GDP (Gross Domestic Product)** — total value of goods/services a country produces. GDP growth/shrinkage signals economic expansion or recession.
- **Recession** — commonly defined as two consecutive quarters of shrinking GDP. Markets often (not always) fall in anticipation of or during recessions.
- **Interest Rates & Central Banks** — in the US, the **Federal Reserve (the Fed)** sets a benchmark interest rate. Raising rates makes borrowing more expensive (cools the economy, often pressures stock prices down); cutting rates does the opposite. This is one of THE biggest market-moving forces — pay attention to Fed announcements.
- **Monetary Policy** — central bank actions (interest rates, money supply) to manage the economy.
- **Fiscal Policy** — government actions (taxes, spending) to manage the economy. Different lever, same goal.
- **Unemployment Rate** — % of workforce without jobs, actively seeking. A key economic health indicator watched closely by markets (jobs reports move prices).

**Market-wide sentiment terms**
- **Bull Market** — sustained period of rising prices / optimism.
- **Bear Market** — sustained period of falling prices (commonly defined as a 20%+ drop from a recent high) / pessimism.
- **Correction** — a shorter, sharper drop (typically 10%+) that doesn't necessarily become a full bear market.
- **Business Cycle** — the recurring pattern of expansion → peak → contraction → trough that economies move through over years.

## 4. The Trader's Lens (terms specific to placing trades)

- **Ticker** — the short code identifying a stock (AAPL = Apple).
- **Bid / Ask / Spread** — bid = best buy offer, ask = best sell offer, spread = the gap between them (a hidden cost of trading).
- **Order types**:
  - **Market order** — execute immediately at current price.
  - **Limit order** — execute only at your specified price or better.
  - **Stop order** — triggers once a price level is hit (used to cap losses = **stop-loss**, or lock in gains = **trailing stop**).
- **Long / Short** — betting on price up / down.
- **Candlestick chart** — shows open/high/low/close per time period; body color shows direction.
- **Support / Resistance** — price floors/ceilings based on past buying/selling pressure.
- **Trend** — the prevailing direction (up/down/sideways); "the trend is your friend" is a common trading maxim.
- **Volume** — number of shares traded; confirms or questions the strength of a price move.
- **Risk/Reward Ratio (R multiple)** — how much you stand to gain vs. lose on a trade, expressed as a ratio to your risk (1R = your initial risk amount).
- **Stop-Loss / Take-Profit** — the predetermined exit points where you cut a loss or lock in a gain — decided *before* you enter, not in the heat of the moment.
